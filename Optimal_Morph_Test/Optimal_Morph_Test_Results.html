Constant-Denominator Slice Experiment: Operator-Linear Morph

This page records a numerical experiment inside a particular region of the Graph Reals \( \mathbb{R}_G \): the constant-denominator slice. The goal is to see how classical network statistics behave along an operator-linear geodesic there, and to clarify exactly what kind of morphing this tests.

1. The Constant-Denominator Slice of \( \mathbb{R}_G \)

The Graph Reals are built as the completion of the field of graph rationals \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) under the Graph–Field Metric, defined via the scaled operator–pair embedding \[ G(q) = \bigl(\Psi(q),\, f(\|\Psi(q)\|)\,\Psi(q)^{-1}\bigr), \] with product norm \(\|(A,B)\|_\times = \|A\|_{\mathrm{op}} + \|B\|_{\mathrm{op}}\).

Constant-denominator slice.

The constant-denominator slice is the subset \[ S := \{\,x/1 \in \mathcal{Q}_G : x \in \mathcal{Z}_G\,\} \subset \mathbb{R}_G. \] Under the operator embedding, this maps to pairs \[ G(x/1) = \bigl(\Psi(x/1),\, f(\|\Psi(x/1)\|)\,\Psi(x/1)^{-1}\bigr), \] and in the operator–pair completion it appears as \[ S \;\cong\; \{ (\Psi(x), \Psi(1)) : x \in \mathcal{Z}_G \}, \] a linear subspace with a fixed second coordinate.

On this slice, each element corresponds to a graph-generated operator \( T_x \) (or its limit) sitting on a uniform denominator background. Step-kernels from finite graphs embed densely into this region, and their associated graphons live inside the usual cut-metric space.

Why this slice is the right setting here.

The experiment concerns:

  • eigenvalues of a normalized adjacency operator \(T\),
  • triangle counts, clustering, and path lengths of sampled graphs,
  • and distances computed from interpolation \(T_t = (1-t)T_0 + tT_1\).

All of these are observables of the operator component \( \Psi(x/1) \) and the graphs it generates on a fixed vertex space. They do not require changes in the denominator geometry, so restricting to the constant denominator slice isolates exactly the operator–level geometry that drives these quantities.

2. Geodesics in the Constant-Denominator Slice

The image of \( S \) under the scaled operator–pair embedding lies inside the Banach space \( \mathcal{B}(H)\times\mathcal{B}(H) \). The distance between two points is \[ d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q,r) = \bigl\|G(q) - G(r)\bigr\|_\times. \] In any normed linear space, straight segments are constant-speed geodesics: for \[ Z(t) = (1-t)Z_0 + t Z_1, \] one has \[ \|Z(t) - Z_0\|_\times = t\,\|Z_1 - Z_0\|_\times, \qquad \|Z(t) - Z_1\|_\times = (1-t)\,\|Z_1 - Z_0\|_\times. \]

Geodesics with fixed denominator.

Inside the constant-denominator slice, the image of \( S \) is a linear subspace of \( \mathcal{B}(H)\times\mathcal{B}(H) \). For any two points represented by operators \(T_0,T_1\) on the same Hilbert space, the path \[ T_t = (1-t)T_0 + t T_1 \] corresponds to a straight segment in the ambient space and therefore realizes a constant-speed geodesic between the two endpoints within this slice.

The experiment uses this geodesic structure as the definition of an optimal morph between two graphs whose denominators (support and measure) remain fixed.

3. Setup of the Numerical Experiment

Two graphs on \( n = 100 \) vertices are chosen:

  • \(G_0\): a \(10 \times 10\) grid (large diameter, very few triangles),
  • \(G_1\): a two-block stochastic block model (short diameter, high clustering).

Each is mapped to a symmetric normalized adjacency operator \[ T_i = D_i^{-1/2} A_i D_i^{-1/2}, \quad i=0,1, \] a finite-dimensional version of the graphon operator \(T_{U_i}\). The operator-linear morph is then \[ T_t = (1-t) T_0 + t T_1,\quad t \in [0,1]. \]

For each \( t \), two things are done:

  • spectral data of \( T_t \) are computed (top eigenvalues),
  • a simple graph is sampled from a rescaled version of \( T_t \) and classical statistics (triangles, clustering, path length) are measured.
What this test is actually checking.
  • That the operator-linear path is behaving as a genuine geodesic between \(T_0\) and \(T_1\) inside the constant-denominator slice (distance to each endpoint varies linearly along the path).
  • How classical network statistics respond along such a geodesic: whether they change smoothly, exhibit sharp transitions, or show structure reminiscent of phase changes.
  • How much of the complexity of “network morphing” is already present in the numerator/operator layer, before any denominator or ghost geometry is introduced.

4. Results

4.1 Spectral Behaviour

The leading eigenvalue sits close to \(1\), as expected for a symmetric normalized adjacency with a dominant stationary mode. The remaining eigenvalues vary smoothly with \(t\) and many follow a U-shaped curve: they decrease from the grid endpoint, reach a minimum in the interior, and then increase again near the SBM endpoint. This behaviour matches the picture of a straight operator path inside the constant-denominator slice: the path is geometrically simple in the Graph–Field metric, yet passes through an intermediate regime with weaker coherent structure.

4.2 Triangles and Clustering

Near \(t=0\), the grid has essentially no triangles and very low clustering. Both statistics remain small for much of the morph, then mount sharply as \(t\) approaches the SBM endpoint. The underlying path in operator space is linear, yet motif counts respond in a strongly curved way.

This highlights a key phenomenon: the Graph–Field geodesic in the constant-denominator slice is spectrally smooth, while classical combinatorial statistics register something much closer to a transition from “lattice-like” to “community-rich” behaviour.

4.3 Average Path Length

The average shortest path length starts around \(8.5\) on the grid and decreases steadily toward about \(2.4\) for the SBM. There is mild sampling noise, but the overall trend moves smoothly downward as the morph progresses.

In geometric terms, the morph collapses a long-diameter lattice into a dense, clustered graph along a constant-speed path in the Graph–Field geometry. At intermediate times one already sees much shorter paths but still modest clustering, reminiscent of small-world effects where distances shrink before clustering fully appears.

4.4 Distances to Endpoints

The curves \( \|T_t - T_0\|_F \) and \( \|T_t - T_1\|_F \) appear as clean straight lines crossing at \( t \approx 0.5 \). Algebraically, \[ T_t - T_0 = t (T_1 - T_0), \qquad T_t - T_1 = (t-1)(T_1 - T_0), \] so \[ \|T_t - T_0\|_F = t\,\|T_1 - T_0\|_F,\qquad \|T_t - T_1\|_F = (1-t)\,\|T_1 - T_0\|_F. \] This is the numerical signature of a constant-speed geodesic segment in the ambient norm.

Relation to the Graph–Field Metric.

The plots use the Frobenius norm for convenience, while the Graph–Field Metric employs the operator norm in the product space. Straight segments enjoy the same linearity property in either normed setting: \(\|Z(t)-Z_0\| = t\|Z_1-Z_0\|\), so this experiment gives direct evidence that the operator-linear path behaves as a genuine geodesic between \(T_0\) and \(T_1\) in the constant-denominator slice.

5. What We Learn From This Slice

  • With denominator fixed, the operator-linear morph \(T_t\) is a clean model of an optimal morph between graphs on a common support in the Graph–Field geometry.
  • Classical observables along this geodesic (spectra, triangles, clustering, path length) show structured behaviour: smooth spectral evolution, sharply increasing clustering, and steadily shrinking average distances.
  • The experiment isolates the contribution of the numerator/operator structure, creating a baseline before introducing denominator geometry and ghost directions. Any additional complexity observed when those are allowed to vary can be understood relative to this flat, constant-denominator starting point.
