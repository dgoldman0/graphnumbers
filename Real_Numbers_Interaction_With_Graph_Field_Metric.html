Graph Reals and the Real Slice: The Graph–Field Metric

This page explains how the Graph Reals \( \mathbb{R}_G \), equipped with the Graph–Field Metric, relate to the usual real line \( \mathbb{R} \). The focus is the embedded “Real slice” \[ \kappa : \mathbb{R} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}_G, \] and the assertion that convergence in \( \mathbb{R}_G \) along this slice matches ordinary Euclidean convergence.

The Graph–Field Metric is induced by a scaled operator–pair embedding of graph rationals into a product of operator spaces. This combines additive information (the operator itself) with multiplicative information (a damped version of the inverse), while still allowing sequences of nonzero graph rationals to converge to \( 0 \).

1. Setup: Graph Rationals and the Graph–Field Metric

Let \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) be the field of graph rationals and let \[ \Psi : \mathcal{Q}_G \longrightarrow \mathcal{B}(H), \quad H = L^2([0,1]), \] be the operator embedding built from regularized graphon kernels and polar decomposition. We view \( \Psi \) as a field homomorphism: \[ \Psi(q + r) = \Psi(q) + \Psi(r),\quad \Psi(qr) = \Psi(q)\Psi(r),\quad \Psi(q^{-1}) = \Psi(q)^{-1}\quad (q \neq 0), \] with \( \Psi(1) = I_H \).

Definition 1.1 (Graph–Field Metric).

Fix a continuous function \( f : [0,\infty) \to [0,\infty) \) satisfying:

  • \( f(0) = 0 \) and \( f(t) > 0 \) for all \( t > 0 \);
  • for each \( \delta > 0 \) there is \( c_\delta > 0 \) such that \( t \ge \delta \Rightarrow f(t) \ge c_\delta \);
  • \( f \) is bounded on \( [0,\infty) \).

A concrete choice is \[ f(t) = \frac{t^2}{1 + t^2}. \]

Define the scaled operator–pair embedding \[ G : \mathcal{Q}_G \to \mathcal{B}(H) \times \mathcal{B}(H) \] by \[ G(q) := \begin{cases} \bigl(\Psi(q),\ f(\|\Psi(q)\|_{\mathrm{op}})\,\Psi(q)^{-1}\bigr), & q \neq 0,\\[4pt] (0,0), & q = 0. \end{cases} \] Equip \( \mathcal{B}(H)\times\mathcal{B}(H) \) with the product norm \[ \|(A,B)\|_{\times} := \|A\|_{\mathrm{op}} + \|B\|_{\mathrm{op}}. \] The Graph–Field Metric on \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) is \[ d_G(q,r) := \bigl\| G(q) - G(r) \bigr\|_{\times}. \]

So \( d_G \) is the metric induced by a scaled operator–pair embedding: it measures both the distance between \( \Psi(q) \) and \( \Psi(r) \), and the distance between their scaled inverses \( f(\|\Psi(q)\|)\Psi(q)^{-1} \) and \( f(\|\Psi(r)\|)\Psi(r)^{-1} \). The scaling \( f \) prevents the inverse coordinate from dominating near \( 0 \), which allows nonzero graph rationals to converge to the Graph Real \( 0 \).

Definition 1.2 (Graph Reals).

A sequence \( (q_n) \subset \mathcal{Q}_G \) is Cauchy if for every \( \varepsilon > 0 \) there exists \( N \) such that \[ n,m \ge N \;\Rightarrow\; d_G(q_n,q_m) < \varepsilon. \] Two Cauchy sequences \( (q_n) \) and \( (r_n) \) are equivalent if \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} d_G(q_n,r_n) = 0. \] The set of equivalence classes is the field of Graph Reals: \[ \mathbb{R}_G := \{[q_n] : (q_n) \text{ Cauchy in } (\mathcal{Q}_G,d_G)\}. \] The distance between two classes is \[ \widehat{d}_G([q_n],[r_n]) := \lim_{n\to\infty} d_G(q_n,r_n). \]

The canonical embedding \[ \iota : \mathcal{Q}_G \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}_G,\quad \iota(q) := [q,q,q,\dots], \] is an isometric field embedding with dense image, and \( (\mathbb{R}_G,\widehat{d}_G) \) is the metric completion of \( (\mathcal{Q}_G,d_G) \).

2. The Real Slice Inside \( \mathbb{R}_G \)

To compare Graph Reals to the usual real line, we choose a distinguished Real slice:

Definition 2.1 (Real slice).

A Real slice embedding is a field embedding \[ \kappa : \mathbb{R} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}_G \] that extends the rational inclusion \( \mathbb{Q} \hookrightarrow \mathcal{Q}_G \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}_G \) and whose operator avatars \[ A(r) := \Psi(\kappa_r) \in \mathcal{B}(H) \] satisfy \[ \|A(r) - A(s)\|_{\mathrm{op}} = |r - s|,\quad \forall r,s \in \mathbb{R}. \]

Intuitively, \( A(r) \) behaves like the scalar operator \( r \cdot I_H \): the difference of two Real-slice elements has operator norm equal to the usual absolute difference of the corresponding real numbers.

On this slice, the Graph–Field Metric becomes \[ d_G(\kappa_r,\kappa_s) = \|A(r) - A(s)\| + \bigl\| f(\|A(r)\|)\,A(r)^{-1} - f(\|A(s)\|)\,A(s)^{-1} \bigr\|, \] interpreted in the obvious way when \( r=0 \) or \( s=0 \). The first term already equals \( |r-s| \), and the second term refines this by comparing a scaled version of the inverse.

3. Convergence on the Real Slice

The key statement is that, restricted to the Real slice, convergence in the Graph–Field Metric agrees with ordinary Euclidean convergence.

Theorem 3.1 (Convergence equivalence on the Real slice).

Let \( (r_n)_{n\ge 1} \subset \mathbb{R} \) and \( r \in \mathbb{R} \). The following are equivalent:

  1. \( r_n \to r \) in the usual Euclidean metric on \( \mathbb{R} \);
  2. \( \kappa_{r_n} \to \kappa_r \) in \( (\mathbb{R}_G,\widehat{d}_G) \) for the Graph–Field Metric.

In other words, the embedding \[ \kappa : (\mathbb{R},|\cdot|) \longrightarrow (\mathbb{R}_G,\widehat{d}_G) \] is a topological embedding onto its image.

3.1 Euclidean convergence implies Graph–Field convergence

Suppose \( r_n \to r \) in \( \mathbb{R} \). Then \[ \|A(r_n) - A(r)\| = |r_n - r| \longrightarrow 0, \] which forces the first coordinate of \( G(\kappa_{r_n}) \) to converge to the first coordinate of \( G(\kappa_r) \).

For the second coordinate, write \[ B(r) := f(\|A(r)\|)\,A(r)^{-1} \quad (r \neq 0), \qquad B(0) := 0. \] On any interval avoiding \( 0 \), continuity of \( A(r) \), \( A(r)^{-1} \), and \( f \) implies \( B(r_n) \to B(r) \). When \( r=0 \), the norms \( \|A(r_n)\| = |r_n| \to 0 \), and the design of \( f \) ensures \[ \|B(r_n)\| = f(|r_n|)\,\|A(r_n)^{-1}\| \longrightarrow 0. \] Thus both coordinates of \( G(\kappa_{r_n}) \) converge to those of \( G(\kappa_r) \), so \[ d_G(\kappa_{r_n},\kappa_r) \to 0. \]

3.2 Graph–Field convergence implies Euclidean convergence

Conversely, assume \( \kappa_{r_n} \to \kappa_r \) in \( (\mathbb{R}_G,\widehat{d}_G) \). Then, by definition of \( d_G \), \[ d_G(\kappa_{r_n},\kappa_r) \;\ge\; \|A(r_n) - A(r)\| \;=\; |r_n - r|. \] The left-hand side tends to zero, so the right-hand side does as well, which means \( r_n \to r \) in the usual Euclidean sense.

This argument also covers the case \( r = 0 \): convergence in \( d_G \) forces \( \|A(r_n) - A(0)\| = |r_n| \to 0, \) hence \( r_n \to 0 \).

4. Quantitative Comparison on Compact Intervals

On compact subsets of the Real slice away from \( 0 \), the Graph–Field Metric and the Euclidean metric are not only topologically equivalent but also comparable up to constants.

Proposition 4.1 (Bi-Lipschitz control on bounded intervals).

Fix \( R > 1 \) and consider \[ I_R := [-R,-1/R] \cup [1/R,R]. \] There exist constants \( c_R, C_R > 0 \) such that for all \( r,s \in I_R \), \[ c_R\,|r-s| \;\le\; d_G(\kappa_r,\kappa_s) \;\le\; C_R\,|r-s|. \]

The lower bound follows immediately from the first coordinate: \[ d_G(\kappa_r,\kappa_s) \ge \|A(r) - A(s)\| = |r-s|. \] For the upper bound, the second coordinate \( f(\|A(r)\|)A(r)^{-1} \) varies continuously with \( r \) on the compact set \( I_R \), so the difference \[ \bigl\| f(\|A(r)\|)A(r)^{-1} - f(\|A(s)\|)A(s)^{-1} \bigr\| \] is bounded by a constant multiple of \( |r-s| \) on \( I_R \). This yields the desired bi-Lipschitz estimate.

Near \( 0 \), the term \( \|A(r)-A(0)\| = |r| \) still gives direct control of distances, so small Real-slice displacements translate directly to small Graph–Field distances.

5. Summary

The Graph–Field Metric on the Graph Reals \( \mathbb{R}_G \) comes from a scaled operator–pair embedding \[ q \longmapsto G(q) = \bigl(\Psi(q), f(\|\Psi(q)\|)\Psi(q)^{-1}\bigr), \] combining the action of \( q \) as an operator with a damped version of its inverse.

On the Real slice \( \kappa(\mathbb{R}) \subset \mathbb{R}_G \), this metric aligns perfectly with ordinary Euclidean geometry:

  • for any real sequence \( (r_n) \) and \( r \in \mathbb{R} \), \[ r_n \to r \iff \kappa_{r_n} \to \kappa_r \text{ in } (\mathbb{R}_G,\widehat{d}_G); \]
  • on bounded intervals away from \( 0 \), the two metrics are bi-Lipschitz equivalent.

So the Graph Reals genuinely extend the usual real line as a topological field: the classical Reals sit inside \( \mathbb{R}_G \) as a slice where limits behave exactly as in standard analysis, while the full Graph–Field Metric continues to capture operator-level and inverse-level structure coming from graph rationals.
