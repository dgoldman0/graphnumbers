Ghost Tracking Cosmology – Model and Testing Strategy

This page defines a simple cosmological model motivated by the Graph Reals framework and its ghost edge sector. Ghost edges are relational degrees of freedom that live in the kernel of an operator map and represent pure relation without geometric location. When coarse-grained over cosmological scales, the ghost sector behaves as an effective fluid:

  • At early times it tracks ordinary matter and looks like an extra dust component.
  • At late times it peels away and behaves like a dark-energy–like component with equation-of-state parameter close to \(-1\).

The goal here is to specify this ghost tracking cosmology cleanly and to outline a testing strategy against cosmological data.

1. Model Overview

We work in a spatially flat FRW universe with scale factor \(a(t)\), Hubble parameter \(H = \dot{a}/a\), and present-day Hubble parameter \(H_0\). Energy densities today are encoded in the usual dimensionless parameters \[ \Omega_r = \frac{\rho_{r0}}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}},\quad \Omega_m = \frac{\rho_{m0}}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}},\quad \Omega_g = \frac{\rho_{g0}}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}}, \] with \[ \rho_{\text{crit},0} = \frac{3H_0^2}{8\pi G} \] and flatness condition \[ \Omega_r + \Omega_m + \Omega_g = 1. \]

The ghost component is interpreted as the coarse-grained energy density of ghost edges in the pregeometric Graph Reals description.

Definition (Ghost energy density). Let \( \bar{\gamma}(a) \) denote the coarse-grained ghost edge content per unit comoving volume at scale factor \( a \). The associated ghost energy density is \[ \rho_g(a) = \Lambda_g \,\bar{\gamma}(a), \] where \( \Lambda_g \) is a fixed microscopic conversion scale.

2. Ghost Tracking Evolution Law

To model how ghost content evolves with the expansion of the universe, we introduce a simple evolution equation in terms of \( \ln a \): \[ \frac{d\bar{\gamma}}{d\ln a} + B\,\bar{\gamma} = A\,\frac{\rho_m(a)}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}}, \quad A,B > 0. \]

Here:

  • The source term \( A\rho_m/\rho_{\text{crit},0} \) describes ghost edges being created wherever matter exists: ghost relations are seeded by ordinary adjacency.
  • The damping term \( -B\bar{\gamma} \) encodes relaxation of ghost content toward a slowly decaying background mode as the universe expands.

For pressureless matter, \[ \rho_m(a) = \rho_{m0} a^{-3} = \Omega_m \rho_{\text{crit},0}\, a^{-3}, \] the evolution equation becomes \[ \frac{d\bar{\gamma}}{d\ln a} + B\,\bar{\gamma} = A\Omega_m a^{-3}. \]

Solving this linear equation yields \[ \bar{\gamma}(a) = \frac{A\Omega_m}{B-3}\,a^{-3} + C\,a^{-B}, \quad B \neq 3, \] with integration constant \(C\) fixed by the present value \( \bar{\gamma}_0 = \bar{\gamma}(1) \). Consequently, \[ \rho_g(a) = \Lambda_g \bar{\gamma}(a) = C_1 a^{-3} + C_2 a^{-B}, \] for suitable constants \(C_1, C_2\).

2.1 Parametrization in Terms of Tracking Fraction

It is convenient to encode the solution using the present ghost density \(\rho_{g0}\) and a tracking fraction \(f_{\text{trk}}\), defined by \[ f_{\text{trk}} := \frac{C_1}{\rho_{g0}},\quad 0 \le f_{\text{trk}} \le 1,\quad \rho_{g0} = C_1 + C_2. \] Then \[ \boxed{ \rho_g(a) = \rho_{g0} \left[ f_{\text{trk}}\,a^{-3} + (1-f_{\text{trk}})\,a^{-B} \right]. } \]

The ghost density in critical units becomes \[ \frac{\rho_g(a)}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}} = \Omega_g \left[ f_{\text{trk}}\,a^{-3} + (1-f_{\text{trk}})\,a^{-B} \right]. \]

3. Background Expansion

The Friedmann equation for a flat universe with radiation, matter, and ghost dark energy reads \[ \frac{H^2(a)}{H_0^2} = \frac{\rho_r(a) + \rho_m(a) + \rho_g(a)}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}}. \]

Using \[ \frac{\rho_r(a)}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}} = \Omega_r a^{-4},\quad \frac{\rho_m(a)}{\rho_{\text{crit},0}} = \Omega_m a^{-3}, \] and the expression for \(\rho_g(a)\), we obtain \[ \boxed{ \frac{H^2(a)}{H_0^2} = \Omega_r a^{-4} + \Omega_m a^{-3} + \Omega_g\left[ f_{\text{trk}}\,a^{-3} + (1-f_{\text{trk}})\,a^{-B} \right]. } \]

Grouping the matter-like terms, \[ \frac{H^2(a)}{H_0^2} = \Omega_r a^{-4} + \underbrace{\big(\Omega_m + f_{\text{trk}}\Omega_g\big)}_{\text{effective matter-like}}\,a^{-3} + \Omega_g(1-f_{\text{trk}})\,a^{-B}. \]

For \( 0 \le B < 3 \), the structure is:

  • Early times: \( a \ll 1 \) ⇒ the \(a^{-3}\) terms dominate; ghost behaves effectively like an additional dust component.
  • Late times: \( a \gg 1 \) ⇒ the \(a^{-B}\) term dominates in the ghost sector; if \(B\) is small, this acts like a slowly varying dark-energy–like component.

4. Effective Ghost Equation of State

One can view the ghost sector as a single effective fluid with energy density \(\rho_g(a)\) and effective equation-of-state parameter \(w_g(a)\) defined by \[ \frac{d\rho_g}{d\ln a} = -3(1+w_g)\rho_g. \]

From \[ \rho_g(a) = \rho_{g0}\left[ f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3} + (1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B} \right], \] we obtain \[ \frac{d\rho_g}{d\ln a} = \rho_{g0}\left[ -3 f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3} - B(1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B} \right]. \]

Setting this equal to \(-3(1+w_g)\rho_g\) gives \[ 1 + w_g(a) = \frac{f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3} + \frac{B}{3}(1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B}} {f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3} + (1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B}}, \] hence \[ \boxed{ w_g(a) = \frac{f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3} + \frac{B}{3}(1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B}} {f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3} + (1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B}} - 1. } \]

Asymptotically:

  • Early universe, \( a \ll 1 \): the term \(f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3}\) dominates in both numerator and denominator, so \[ w_g(a \ll 1) \approx 0. \] Ghost energy behaves like dust.
  • Late universe, \( a \gg 1 \): the term \((1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B}\) dominates, giving \[ w_g(a \gg 1) \approx \frac{B}{3} - 1 = -1 + \frac{B}{3}. \]

Define the asymptotic late-time value \[ w_{g,\infty} := -1 + \frac{B}{3},\quad 0 \le B < 3. \] One can invert this as \[ B = 3(1 + w_{g,\infty}), \] and use \(w_{g,\infty}\) rather than \(B\) as a primary parameter for late-time behavior.

5. Parameter Set

A convenient set of parameters for the ghost tracking model is:

  • Usual cosmological parameters: \( \Omega_r, \Omega_m, H_0 \).
  • Ghost sector parameters:
    • \( \Omega_g \): present-day ghost density fraction.
    • \( f_{\text{trk}} \): fraction of ghost energy that historically tracked matter.
    • \( w_{g,\infty} \): late-time asymptotic equation-of-state value.

These satisfy \[ \Omega_r + \Omega_m + \Omega_g = 1, \] with \(0 \le f_{\text{trk}} \le 1\) and \( -1 \le w_{g,\infty} < 0 \) (for \(0 \le B < 3\)), if one wants a late-time component that interpolates between dust-like and dark-energy–like behavior.

6. Testing Strategy

The model is now specific enough to be constrained and potentially falsified by cosmological data. The key idea is that the ghost sector obeys a highly restricted evolution:

  • It behaves like extra dust in the early universe.
  • It transitions smoothly to a dark-energy–like component with fixed asymptotic \(w_{g,\infty}\).
  • The transition is controlled by just two parameters, \(f_{\text{trk}}\) and \(w_{g,\infty}\), rather than an arbitrary function \(w(z)\).

6.1 Early-Universe Constraints (CMB and BBN)

At early times (\(a \ll 1\), ignoring radiation for the moment), ghost energy behaves like dust: \[ \rho_g(a) \approx \rho_{g0} f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3}. \]

The effective matter-like density is then \[ \rho_{\text{m,eff}}(a) \approx \big(\Omega_m + f_{\text{trk}}\Omega_g\big)\rho_{\text{crit},0}\,a^{-3}. \]

Precision measurements of the early universe (CMB anisotropies, Big Bang nucleosynthesis) tightly constrain the effective matter density at high redshift. This yields bounds on the combination \(\Omega_m + f_{\text{trk}}\Omega_g\), thus limiting the allowed early tracking fraction \(f_{\text{trk}}\) for a given \(\Omega_g\).

Early-universe test: If the inferred early-time matter density from CMB/BBN excludes any reasonable \(\Omega_m + f_{\text{trk}}\Omega_g\) combination compatible with late-time fits, the ghost tracking model (in this form) is ruled out.

6.2 Background Expansion (SN, BAO, CMB Distance)

The Hubble history \[ \frac{H^2(a)}{H_0^2} = \Omega_r a^{-4} + \big(\Omega_m + f_{\text{trk}}\Omega_g\big)a^{-3} + \Omega_g(1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B} \] determines luminosity distances for supernovae, angular-diameter distances for BAO, and the distance to the surface of last scattering in the CMB.

Observations can be used to fit the parameters \((\Omega_g, f_{\text{trk}}, w_{g,\infty})\) and to check:

  • Whether the data prefer a nonzero \(\Omega_g\) and a late-time \(w_{g,\infty} \neq -1\).
  • Whether the specific functional form \(\rho_g(a) \propto f_{\text{trk}} a^{-3} + (1-f_{\text{trk}})a^{-B}\) can fit distance data as well as or better than \(\Lambda\)CDM or generic \(w\)CDM.
  • Whether the smooth, monotonic interpolation of \(w_g(a)\) from \(0\) to \(w_{g,\infty}\) is compatible with reconstructed \(w(z)\).
Background test: If distance data prefer a time variation of dark energy that lies outside the smooth, two-parameter family implied by the ghost tracking law, the model is disfavored.

6.3 Growth of Structure

The ghost sector modifies the effective matter content and late-time expansion history, which in turn affect the growth of linear perturbations. Schematically, the growth rate \(f\sigma_8(z)\) depends on:

  • The early-time effective matter fraction \(\Omega_m + f_{\text{trk}}\Omega_g\).
  • The detailed expansion history governed by \(\Omega_g, f_{\text{trk}}\), and \(w_{g,\infty}\).

A distinctive feature of the tracking model is that early extra “dust-like” ghost content and late-time dark-energy–like ghost content are not independent; they are linked through a single fluid with a specific \(w_g(a)\).

Growth test: Compare the model’s predicted growth history, derived from the above \(H(a)\) and effective matter fraction, with redshift-space distortions and weak lensing data. If the combination of background and growth data cannot be fit by any \((\Omega_g, f_{\text{trk}}, w_{g,\infty})\), the ghost tracking hypothesis in this minimal form is ruled out.

6.4 Distinctive Signatures

In summary, the ghost tracking model makes the following qualitative predictions:

  • Early extra dust: a small, matter-like contribution to the early universe energy budget from ghost edges, parameterized by \(f_{\text{trk}}\Omega_g\).
  • Smooth interpolation of \(w_g(a)\): the ghost equation of state transitions monotonically from \(w_g \approx 0\) at high redshift to \(w_{g,\infty} = -1 + B/3\) at late times.
  • Coupled early and late behavior: the amount of early tracking and the late-time dark-energy behavior are tied together by a single evolution law, rather than being independent free functions.
  • Coincidence framing: the onset of acceleration is linked to the dilution of matter and the peeling off of the \(a^{-B}\) mode, providing a relational explanation for why dark energy becomes important only when matter has diluted sufficiently.

These features distinguish ghost tracking cosmology from both pure \(\Lambda\)CDM and completely generic dynamical dark-energy models.

7. Outlook

The ghost tracking model provides a concrete, minimal bridge between pregeometric ghost edges in Graph Reals and cosmological observables. It replaces a generic dark-energy fluid with a single structured component whose early and late behavior are linked through a simple evolution law.

Future work can extend this framework to:

  • Include perturbations in the ghost sector explicitly and track their impact on structure formation.
  • Derive the effective parameters \(f_{\text{trk}}\) and \(w_{g,\infty}\) from microscopic dynamics in the Graph Reals algebra, rather than treating them as free.
  • Explore connections between ghost tracking behavior and other regimes, such as black holes and the early universe, within the same pregeometric picture.

As observational data continue to sharpen, this model offers a clear and testable way to ask whether the dark sector could indeed be the large-scale shadow of relational degrees of freedom that standard geometric operators do not see.
