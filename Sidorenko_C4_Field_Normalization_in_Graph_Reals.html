Sidorenko for C₄ in Graph Reals: Normalizing Inside the Field

This page illustrates Strategy A for Sidorenko’s conjecture inside the Graph Reals framework, using the 4-cycle \( C_4 \) as a test case and normalizing entirely inside the field \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

Other pages in this series describe the construction of the Graph Reals \( \mathbb{R}_G \) from graph rationals \( \mathcal{Q}_G \), the Graph–Field metric \( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \), the Real slice, and the ghost edge. Here the goal is narrower:

  • Recast Sidorenko’s inequality for \( C_4 \) using field normalization.
  • Write the normalized problem on a single level set in \( \mathbb{R}_G \).
  • Compute the first and second variation near the constant Graph Real.

The outcome is a clean local picture: in normalized coordinates the constant Graph Real is a critical point of the \( C_4 \)-density functional, and the second variation is a positive quadratic form related to the operator embedding. This is a small but concrete glimpse of why Strategy A has genuine bite.

1. Setup: Graphons, Graph Reals, and Operators

Let \( X \in \mathbb{R}_G \) be a graphon-type Graph Real, arising as the limit (in the Graph–Field metric) of a Cauchy sequence of step-graph rationals whose kernel operators converge to a symmetric kernel \( W_X : [0,1]^2 \to \mathbb{R} \).

The operator embedding from the Graph Reals construction assigns \[ T_X := \Psi(X), \] a bounded self-adjoint operator on \( L^2([0,1]) \) whose integral kernel is \( W_X \) along the graphon slice. The Graph–Field metric \( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \) is defined through \[ G(q) = \bigl(\Psi(q),\, f(\|\Psi(q)\|)\Psi(q)^{-1}\bigr), \] and completion of \( (\mathcal{Q}_G, d_{\mathrm{GF}}) \) yields the field \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

On the constant-denominator slice \( S = \{ x/1 : x \in \mathcal{Z}_G \} \), the Graph–Field metric dominates both the operator norm and the graphon cut metric. Convergence in \( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \) therefore implies convergence in the usual graphon sense along this slice.

1.1. Homomorphism densities in operator form

For a graphon \( W_X \) attached to \( X \), let

  • The edge density \[ p(X) := t(K_2,X) = \iint W_X(x,y)\,dx\,dy. \]
  • The \( C_4 \)-density \[ t(C_4,X) = \int W_X(x_1,x_2)W_X(x_2,x_3) W_X(x_3,x_4)W_X(x_4,x_1)\,dx_1\cdots dx_4. \]

On the operator side this satisfies the familiar identity \[ t(C_4,X) = \mathrm{tr}(T_X^4), \] since expanding the trace of \( T_X^4 \) over an orthonormal basis yields the same 4-fold integral as the graphon definition of \( t(C_4,\cdot) \).

2. Field Normalization for Sidorenko

Sidorenko’s inequality for \( C_4 \) in graphon form reads:

Sidorenko for \( C_4 \) (graphon version). For every graphon \( W \), \[ t(C_4,W) \;\ge\; t(K_2,W)^4. \]

In terms of Graph Reals, this becomes a statement about \( X \in \mathbb{R}_G \) whose kernel is \( W_X \). The Sidorenko ratio \[ R_{C_4}(X) := \frac{t(C_4,X)}{p(X)^4} \] is well-defined whenever \( p(X)=t(K_2,X)>0 \).

2.1. Multiplying by the Real slice

The Real slice embedding \( \kappa:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}_G \) from the Graph Reals construction satisfies \[ \Psi(\kappa_a) = a I_{L^2} \] for every scalar \( a \). Multiplying a graphon-type Graph Real \( X \) by \( \kappa_a \) scales its operator by \( a \): \[ \Psi(\kappa_a X) = \Psi(\kappa_a)\Psi(X) = a\,T_X. \]

For homomorphism densities this gives homogeneous scaling:

  • Each edge in \( K_2 \) contributes one factor of \( a \), so \[ t(K_2,\kappa_a X) = a\,t(K_2,X). \]
  • Each edge in \( C_4 \) contributes one factor of \( a \), so \[ t(C_4,\kappa_a X) = a^4\,t(C_4,X). \]

This is the key homogeneity property that Strategy A uses, now realized as an internal operation of \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

2.2. Normalized Graph Reals

Let \( X\in\mathbb{R}_G \) be graphon-type with \[ p(X) := t(K_2,X) > 0. \] Define the normalized Graph Real \[ Y := \kappa_{p(X)^{-1}}\cdot X. \] The edge density of \( Y \) is \[ t(K_2,Y) = t(K_2,\kappa_{p^{-1}}X) = p(X)^{-1}\,t(K_2,X) = 1. \] The \( C_4 \)-density scales as \[ t(C_4,Y) = t(C_4,\kappa_{p^{-1}}X) = p(X)^{-4} t(C_4,X) = R_{C_4}(X). \]

Normalized cone. Let \[ \mathcal{C} := \{\, Y\in\mathbb{R}_G : Y \text{ is graphon-type and } t(K_2,Y)=1 \,\} \] be the normalized level set for the edge density functional.

Sidorenko’s inequality for \( C_4 \) is therefore equivalent to:

Sidorenko for \( C_4 \) (field-normalized form). For every graphon-type \( Y \in \mathcal{C} \), \[ t(C_4,Y) \ge 1. \] In particular, the constant Graph Real \( Y_\star \in \mathcal{C} \) with kernel \( W\equiv 1 \) satisfies \[ t(C_4,Y) \ge t(C_4,Y_\star) = 1. \]

The constant kernel \( W \equiv 1 \) has \[ t(K_2,W)=1,\quad t(C_4,W)=1, \] so its Graph Real avatar \( Y_\star \) is the canonical base point in the normalized cone \( \mathcal{C} \).

3. Local Coordinates Around the Constant Graph Real

To probe the geometry of \( t(C_4,\cdot) \) on \( \mathcal{C} \), consider a small perturbation around \( Y_\star \). On the kernel side, write \[ W_Y(x,y) = 1 + \varepsilon f(x,y), \] where \( f \) is symmetric and \( \varepsilon \) is a small parameter.

3.1. Edge-density constraint

The constraint \( t(K_2,Y) = 1 \) reads \[ 1 = t(K_2,Y) = \iint (1+\varepsilon f(x,y))\,dx\,dy = 1 + \varepsilon \iint f(x,y)\,dx\,dy + O(\varepsilon^2). \] Hence the first-order condition is \[ \iint f(x,y)\,dx\,dy = 0. \]

Tangent directions to \( \mathcal{C} \) at \( Y_\star \) therefore correspond to symmetric functions \( f \) with zero global mean. These are precisely the perturbations that preserve edge density at first order.

3.2. Operator representation

On the operator side, \( W_Y = 1+\varepsilon f \) corresponds to \[ T_Y = T_1 + \varepsilon T_f, \] where:

  • \( T_1: L^2\to L^2 \) is the rank-one operator \[ (T_1 g)(x) = \int g(y)\,dy, \] projection onto the constant functions with eigenvalue 1.
  • \( T_f \) is the Hilbert–Schmidt operator with kernel \( f \).

The \( C_4 \)-density satisfies \[ t(C_4,Y) = \mathrm{tr}(T_Y^4) = \mathrm{tr}\bigl( (T_1+\varepsilon T_f)^4 \bigr). \]

4. First and Second Variation of \( t(C_4,Y) \)

Expanding \( T_Y^4 \) as a binomial in \( T_1 \) and \( T_f \) gives \[ T_Y^4 = T_1^4 + 4\varepsilon T_1^3 T_f + 6\varepsilon^2 T_1^2 T_f^2 + 4\varepsilon^3 T_1 T_f^3 + \varepsilon^4 T_f^4. \] Taking traces yields \[ t(C_4,Y) = \mathrm{tr}(T_1^4) + 4\varepsilon\,\mathrm{tr}(T_1^3 T_f) + 6\varepsilon^2\,\mathrm{tr}(T_1^2 T_f^2) + O(\varepsilon^3). \]

4.1. Zeroth and first order

The zeroth-order term is \[ \mathrm{tr}(T_1^4) = \mathrm{tr}(T_1) = 1, \] which matches \( t(C_4,Y_\star)=1 \).

For the first variation, note that \( T_1^3 = T_1 \), so \[ \mathrm{tr}(T_1^3 T_f) = \mathrm{tr}(T_1 T_f). \] In an orthonormal basis with \( e_0 \) proportional to the constant function and the remaining basis vectors orthogonal to it, one has \[ T_1 = | e_0 \rangle \langle e_0 |. \] The condition \( \iint f = 0 \) implies \[ \langle e_0, T_f e_0 \rangle = \iint f(x,y)\,dx\,dy = 0, \] and therefore \[ \mathrm{tr}(T_1 T_f) = \langle e_0, T_f e_0 \rangle = 0. \]

Hence the first-order term vanishes: \[ \frac{d}{d\varepsilon} t(C_4,Y)\bigg|_{\varepsilon=0} = 0. \] The constant Graph Real \( Y_\star \) is a stationary point of \( t(C_4,\cdot) \) on the normalized cone \( \mathcal{C} \).

4.2. Second order

The second-order term involves \[ 6\varepsilon^2\,\mathrm{tr}(T_1^2 T_f^2) = 6\varepsilon^2\,\mathrm{tr}(T_1 T_f^2), \] since \( T_1^2 = T_1 \). Using the same basis, \[ \mathrm{tr}(T_1 T_f^2) = \langle e_0, T_f^2 e_0 \rangle = \| T_f e_0 \|_2^2 \ge 0. \]

Therefore \[ \frac{d^2}{d\varepsilon^2} t(C_4,Y)\bigg|_{\varepsilon=0} = 12 \, \| T_f e_0 \|_2^2 \ge 0. \]

Second variation at the constant Graph Real. On the normalized cone \( \mathcal{C} \), the constant Graph Real \( Y_\star \) is a stationary point of the functional \( Y \mapsto t(C_4,Y) \). The second variation in tangent directions given by zero-mean kernels \( f \) is \[ \delta^2 t(C_4)[f] = 12 \, \| T_f e_0 \|_2^2 \ge 0, \] with equality precisely when \( T_f e_0 = 0 \).

This yields a local minimality statement: in a Graph–Field neighborhood of \( Y_\star \) inside \( \mathcal{C} \), any small normalized perturbation can only increase \( t(C_4,\cdot) \) up to second order.

5. How Strategy A Appears in This Calculation

Several aspects of this computation highlight the advantages of normalizing inside \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

  • Internal normalization. Edge density is absorbed into the field via the Real slice. The normalized element \[ Y = \kappa_{p(X)^{-1}} X \] lies on the level set \( t(K_2,Y)=1 \). The Sidorenko ratio for \( C_4 \) becomes simply \[ R_{C_4}(X) = t(C_4,Y), \] so Sidorenko is a minimization statement for a single functional on the normalized hypersurface \( \mathcal{C} \).
  • Single base point with clean operator avatar. The constant graphon \( W\equiv 1 \) corresponds to a canonical Graph Real \( Y_\star\in\mathcal{C} \) with operator avatar \( T_1 = |e_0\rangle\langle e_0| \). It sits at the center of this normalized picture, and the local expansion of \( t(C_4,\cdot) \) around it uses exactly the operators that define the Graph–Field metric.
  • Operator-based variation compatible with the metric. The perturbation \( T_Y = T_1 + \varepsilon T_f \) lives naturally in the operator coordinate \( \Psi(X) \), and the second variation involves \( \|T_f e_0\|_2^2 \), a quantity handled comfortably by the analytic side of the Graph–Field setup.

For \( C_4 \), the full Sidorenko inequality is known by direct analytic arguments, so this computation serves as a consistency check and a demonstration of technique. Similar expansions for other bipartite graphs \( H \) may reveal patterns in the second variation that relate to convexity or majorization properties within the normalized cone \( \mathcal{C} \).

6. Outlook

This worked example shows how Strategy A packages Sidorenko’s inequality into the geometry of a functional on a normalized hypersurface inside \( \mathbb{R}_G \). For \( C_4 \), the constant Graph Real is a local minimum of \( t(C_4,\cdot) \) on \( \mathcal{C} \), with a positive second variation expressed in terms of the operator embedding.

The broader program is to:

  • Extend this analysis to general bipartite \( H \), tracking \( t(H,\cdot) \) along normalized Graph Reals.
  • Seek geodesic or convexity properties of \( \log t(H,\cdot) \) along natural paths in \( \mathcal{C} \) defined through the Graph–Field metric.
  • Combine these properties with the scalar–ghost decomposition from the ghost-edge page to separate “random baseline” behavior from structured contributions.

Even modest convexity results inside this framework would give fresh perspectives on Sidorenko-type inequalities, now phrased as geometric statements in the field of Graph Reals.
