From Clique to Clouds: Graph Reals via the Graph–Field Metric

This page summarizes a unified algebraic–analytic framework that extends finite graph arithmetic to a completed field of Graph Reals, built from operator-theoretic data. The goal is to extend counting and spectral information from finite graphs to continuous “clouds” (graphons and their operator avatars) while keeping a genuine field structure in the background.

1. Combinatorial–Algebraic Layers

  • Graph Naturals \( \mathcal{N}_G \): Isomorphism classes of finite simple graphs, with disjoint union \( \sqcup \) and Cartesian product \( \Box \), forming a commutative semiring.
  • Graph Integers \( \mathcal{Z}_G \): Grothendieck completion of \( \mathcal{N}_G \), forming a ring of formal differences \( [G] - [H] \).
  • Graph Rationals \( \mathcal{Q}_G \): Field of fractions of \( \mathcal{Z}_G \), with elements \( x/y \) for \( x, y \in \mathcal{Z}_G \), \( y \neq 0 \).

Intuitively, \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) is the smallest field in which we can divide graph differences by each other while preserving the combinatorial operations inherited from the semiring and ring layers.

2. Operator–Kernel Embedding

Each finite graph (or formal difference) gives rise to a regularized step-kernel operator on \( H := L^2([0,1]) \).

For a graph \( G \) on \( n \) vertices with adjacency matrix \( A_G \), partition \( [0,1] \) into intervals \( I_i := [(i-1)/n, i/n) \). Define the raw step kernel \[ W_G^0(x,y) := \sum_{i,j=1}^n A_G(i,j)\,\mathbf{1}_{I_i}(x)\,\mathbf{1}_{I_j}(y) \] and then regularize via a “loop shift” \[ W_G(x,y) = W_G^0(x,y) + \lambda_G\,\mathbf{1}_{I_i \times I_i}(x,y), \] where \( \lambda_G > -\min \operatorname{spec}(A_G) \) is chosen so the associated operator \( T_{W_G} : H \to H \) is strictly positive and invertible.

For a graph integer \( x = [G] - [H] \in \mathcal{Z}_G \), define \[ T_x := T_{W_G} - T_{W_H}. \] For a graph rational element \( x/y \in \mathcal{Q}_G \), with \( x,y \in \mathcal{Z}_G \), we write the polar decomposition \( T_y = U_y |T_y| \) and define the operator embedding \[ \Psi\!\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) := |T_y|^{-1/2} U_y^*\, T_x\, U_y\, |T_y|^{-1/2}. \]

The construction is arranged so that:

  • \( \Psi \) is a ring homomorphism \( \mathcal{Z}_G \to \mathcal{B}(H) \),
  • \( \Psi \) extends to a field embedding \( \mathcal{Q}_G \hookrightarrow \mathcal{B}(H) \) by sending \( (x,y) \mapsto \Psi(x)\Psi(y)^{-1} \) in a way compatible with the polar-decomposition normalization above,
  • \( \Psi \) is injective, so operator equality reflects equality in \( \mathcal{Q}_G \).

In short, each graph rational \( q\in\mathcal{Q}_G \) has an operator avatar \( \Psi(q)\in\mathcal{B}(H) \) that respects the field operations.

3. Graphons and the Cut-Metric Closure

The graphon space \( \mathcal{W} \) consists of symmetric measurable kernels \( U:[0,1]^2\to\mathbb{R} \), modulo equality almost everywhere and modulo measure-preserving relabeling. Equipped with the cut-metric \[ d_\square(U, V) := \sup_{S,T \subset [0,1]} \left| \int_{S\times T} (U - V) \right|, \] the space \( (\mathcal{W}, d_\square) \) is a complete metric space.

Step-kernels from finite graphs embed densely into \( \mathcal{W} \), and \( T_U \) denotes the integral operator associated to a graphon \( U \). There is a close link between the operator norm and the cut norm, but the graphon space on its own carries only an additive metric structure; there is no multiplicative structure compatible with \( d_\square \).

The image \( \Psi(\mathcal{Q}_G) \) lies densely in a Banach subspace \( \mathcal{F}_G \subset \mathcal{B}(H) \) of “graph-generated operators”, preserving addition and multiplication but not necessarily forming a closed field under inversion.

4. Graph–Field Metric and Graph Reals

We now introduce a metric on \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) that captures both additive and multiplicative geometry through the operator embedding \( \Psi \), and then complete \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) with respect to this metric to obtain the Graph Reals.

4.1 Definition of the Graph–Field Metric

The goal is to measure how close two graph rationals are by comparing both their operators and their operator inverses, while allowing sequences of graph rationals to converge to \( 0 \) in the natural operator sense.

Fix a continuous function \[ f : [0,\infty) \to [0,\infty) \] with the following properties:

  • \( f(0)=0 \), and \( f(t)>0 \) for all \( t>0 \);
  • for every \( \delta>0 \), there exists \( c_\delta>0 \) with \( t \ge \delta \Rightarrow f(t)\ge c_\delta \);
  • \( f \) is bounded: there exists \( M>0 \) with \( f(t)\le M \) for all \( t\ge 0 \).

A concrete choice is \[ f(t) := \frac{t^2}{1+t^2}. \]

Define the scaled operator–pair embedding \[ G : \mathcal{Q}_G \to \mathcal{B}(H)\times\mathcal{B}(H) \] by \[ G(q) := \begin{cases} \big(\Psi(q),\, f(\|\Psi(q)\|_{\mathrm{op}})\,\Psi(q)^{-1}\big), & q \neq 0,\\[6pt] (0,0), & q = 0. \end{cases} \] Equip \( \mathcal{B}(H)\times\mathcal{B}(H) \) with the product norm \[ \|(A,B)\|_{\times} := \|A\|_{\mathrm{op}} + \|B\|_{\mathrm{op}}. \] The Graph–Field Metric on \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) is then \[ d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q,r) := \big\|G(q) - G(r)\big\|_{\times}. \]

This is a scaled operator–pair metric: when \( q,r \) stay away from \( 0 \), it essentially reduces to comparing both \( \Psi(q) \) and \( \Psi(q^{-1}) \) to their counterparts for \( r \); when \( q \to 0 \), the second coordinate is damped by the factor \( f(\|\Psi(q)\|) \), so sequences can converge to \( 0 \) in the usual operator sense.

Lemma 4.1 (Metric).

\( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \) is a metric on \( \mathcal{Q}_G \).

Proof.

Symmetry and the triangle inequality follow because \( d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q,r) = \|G(q)-G(r)\|_{\times} \) is the pullback of a norm on a linear space via the map \( G \). To see definiteness, suppose \( d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q,r)=0 \). Then \( G(q)=G(r) \).

  • If both are nonzero, then \( \Psi(q)=\Psi(r) \) and \( f(\|\Psi(q)\|)\Psi(q)^{-1} = f(\|\Psi(r)\|)\Psi(r)^{-1} \). Because \( f(\|\Psi(q)\|)>0 \), the first equality implies \( q=r \) by injectivity of \( \Psi \).
  • If exactly one of \( q,r \) is zero, then the first coordinates \( \Psi(q) \) and \( \Psi(r) \) differ, so \( G(q)\neq G(r) \).

Hence \( d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q,r)=0 \) only when \( q=r \). \(\square\)

We may therefore regard \( (\mathcal{Q}_G, d_{\mathrm{GF}}) \) as a metric subset of the Banach space \( (\mathcal{B}(H)^2,\|\cdot\|_{\times}) \) via the embedding \( G \).

4.2 Completion: the Graph Reals

A sequence \( (q_n) \) in \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) is Cauchy in the Graph–Field metric if for every \( \varepsilon>0 \) there exists \( N \) such that \[ n,m \ge N \;\Rightarrow\; d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q_n,q_m) < \varepsilon. \] Two Cauchy sequences \( (q_n) \) and \( (r_n) \) are equivalent if \[ \lim_{n\to\infty} d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q_n,r_n) = 0. \]

The set of equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences is the space of Graph Reals, denoted \[ \mathbb{R}_G := \{ [q_n] : (q_n)\text{ Cauchy in }(\mathcal{Q}_G,d_{\mathrm{GF}})\}. \] For two classes \( [q_n] \) and \( [r_n] \), define \[ \widehat{d}_{\mathrm{GF}}([q_n],[r_n]) := \lim_{n\to\infty} d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q_n,r_n), \] which exists and does not depend on the choice of representatives.

Lemma 4.2 (Completeness).

\( (\mathbb{R}_G, \widehat{d}_{\mathrm{GF}}) \) is a complete metric space.

Proof.

This is the standard Cauchy-sequence completion construction: by definition, \( \mathbb{R}_G \) consists of Cauchy sequences modulo the relation “distance tends to \(0\)”, and \( \widehat{d}_{\mathrm{GF}} \) is the usual quotient metric. Every Cauchy sequence of classes admits a diagonal representative that converges, so every Cauchy sequence in \( \mathbb{R}_G \) has a limit. \(\square\)

Lemma 4.3 (Canonical embedding).

The map \[ \iota : \mathcal{Q}_G \to \mathbb{R}_G,\quad \iota(q) := [q,q,q,\dots], \] is an isometric embedding with dense image.

Proof.

For \( q,r \in \mathcal{Q}_G \), \[ \widehat{d}_{\mathrm{GF}}(\iota(q),\iota(r)) = \lim_{n\to\infty} d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q,r) = d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q,r), \] so \( \iota \) preserves distances and is injective. Density follows because every Cauchy sequence \( (q_n) \) has \( \iota(q_n) \to [q_n] \) in \( \mathbb{R}_G \) by construction. \(\square\)

We identify \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) with its image in \( \mathbb{R}_G \) and think of \( \mathbb{R}_G \) as the Graph–Field completion of \( \mathcal{Q}_G \).

4.3 Field Operations and Topological Field Structure

We now extend the field structure from \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) to \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

For \( [q_n],[r_n] \in \mathbb{R}_G \), define:

  • Addition: \[ [q_n] + [r_n] := [q_n + r_n]. \]
  • Additive inverse: \[ -[q_n] := [-q_n]. \]
  • Multiplication: \[ [q_n]\cdot[r_n] := [q_n r_n]. \]
  • Multiplicative inverse: if \( [q_n]\neq 0 \), choose a representative with \( q_n\neq 0 \) for all large \( n \), and set \[ [q_n]^{-1} := [q_n^{-1}]. \]
Lemma 4.4 (Well-defined operations).

Addition, additive inverse, multiplication, and inversion as defined above do not depend on the choice of representatives of the equivalence classes.

Proof.

Suppose \( (q_n)\sim(q_n') \) and \( (r_n)\sim(r_n') \). Then \( d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q_n,q_n')\to 0 \) and \( d_{\mathrm{GF}}(r_n,r_n')\to 0 \), which means \( G(q_n)\to G(q_n') \) and \( G(r_n)\to G(r_n') \) in the ambient Banach space \( \mathcal{B}(H)^2 \).

The field operations on \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) can be expressed at the operator level as continuous functions of \( G(q) \) and \( G(r) \):

  • For addition, the first coordinate of \( G(q+r) \) is \( \Psi(q+r)=\Psi(q)+\Psi(r) \), and the second coordinate is \( f(\|\Psi(q+r)\|)\Psi(q+r)^{-1} \), both continuous in \( \Psi(q),\Psi(r) \).
  • For multiplication, \( \Psi(qr)=\Psi(q)\Psi(r) \) and \( (qr)^{-1}=r^{-1}q^{-1} \), so \( G(qr) \) depends continuously on \( G(q),G(r) \).
  • For inversion, on the nonzero locus \( G(q^{-1}) \) is given by a continuous transformation of \( G(q) \), involving \( \Psi(q)^{-1} \) and \( \Psi(q) \).

Since \( G(q_n)\to G(q_n') \) and \( G(r_n)\to G(r_n') \), continuity of these operator-level maps implies: \[ G(q_n + r_n) - G(q_n' + r_n') \to 0,\quad G(q_n r_n) - G(q_n' r_n') \to 0,\quad G(q_n^{-1}) - G(q_n'^{-1}) \to 0 \] wherever the operations are defined. That is, \[ d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q_n+r_n, q_n'+r_n') \to 0,\quad d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q_n r_n, q_n' r_n') \to 0,\quad d_{\mathrm{GF}}(q_n^{-1}, q_n'^{-1}) \to 0. \]

Hence \( (q_n + r_n)\sim(q_n'+r_n') \), \( (q_n r_n)\sim(q_n' r_n') \), and \( (q_n^{-1})\sim(q_n'^{-1}) \), so the operations on \( \mathbb{R}_G \) are well-defined. \(\square\)

Proposition 4.5 (Field structure).

With the above operations, \( \mathbb{R}_G \) is a field, and \( \iota:\mathcal{Q}_G\hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}_G \) is a field embedding with dense image.

Proof.

The field axioms (associativity, commutativity of addition and multiplication, distributivity, existence of additive and multiplicative identities and inverses, etc.) hold in \( \mathcal{Q}_G \). Because operations on \( \mathbb{R}_G \) are defined termwise on representatives, these identities pass to equivalence classes.

The embedding \( \iota \) respects all operations by construction: \( \iota(q+r)=\iota(q)+\iota(r) \), \( \iota(qr)=\iota(q)\iota(r) \), and so on. Its injectivity and density follow from Lemma 4.3. \(\square\)

Theorem 4.6 (Topological field).

Equipped with the metric \( \widehat{d}_{\mathrm{GF}} \) and the operations defined above, \( \mathbb{R}_G \) is a complete topological field: addition, subtraction, multiplication, and inversion on \( \mathbb{R}_G^\times \) are continuous maps.

Proof.

On \( \mathcal{Q}_G \), the operations are continuous with respect to \( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \), since they are obtained by restricting continuous maps on the Banach space \( \mathcal{B}(H)^2 \) to the embedded subset \( G(\mathcal{Q}_G) \).

The completion construction of \( \mathbb{R}_G \) ensures that any continuous operation on a dense subspace that sends Cauchy sequences to Cauchy sequences and respects equivalence extends uniquely to a continuous operation on the completion. Lemma 4.4 verifies the Cauchy and equivalence-preserving properties for addition, multiplication, and inversion, so these extend to continuous operations on \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

Completeness is Lemma 4.2. Thus \( \mathbb{R}_G \) is a complete topological field. \(\square\)

5. Graphon Subspace and Metric Domination

The constant-denominator slice \[ S := \{\; x/1 \in \mathcal{Q}_G : x\in\mathcal{Z}_G \;\} \] maps under \( \Psi \) to regularized step-kernels and their limits, which embed into the graphon space \( \mathcal{W} \) via associated kernels \( W_x \). On this slice, the Graph–Field metric dominates the cut-metric: if \( U,V \) are graphons corresponding to elements of \( S \), \[ d_\square(U,V) \;\le\; \|T_U - T_V\|_{\mathrm{op}} \;\le\; d_{\mathrm{GF}}(x/1, y/1). \]

Therefore, convergence in the Graph Reals along this slice implies convergence in the graphon cut-metric. This allows operator-level and graphon-level limits to be studied simultaneously within a single completed field.

6. Real Line and Spectral Interpretation

One expects to embed a copy of the usual real line \( \mathbb{R} \) into \( \mathbb{R}_G \) by identifying scalars with appropriate “scalar graph ratios”. For example, a one-parameter family \( r \mapsto \kappa_r \in \mathcal{Q}_G \) can be chosen so that:

  • \( \kappa_r + \kappa_s = \kappa_{r+s} \),
  • \( \kappa_r \cdot \kappa_s = \kappa_{rs} \),
  • and the restriction of \( \widehat{d}_{\mathrm{GF}} \) to \( \{\kappa_r : r\in\mathbb{R}\} \) agrees with the usual absolute value \( |r-s| \) up to a fixed multiplicative constant.

On such slices, the metric and field structure recover the familiar real number line, while on more general elements of \( \mathbb{R}_G \) they encode higher-order spectral and combinatorial information.

More generally, the operator coordinates \( \Psi(q) \) and \( f(\|\Psi(q)\|)\Psi(q)^{-1} \) of a Graph Real \( q \in \mathbb{R}_G^\times \) record global spectral features (eigenvalue distribution, condition number, etc.) of the underlying graph ratios, suggesting a spectral interpretation of numerical invariants computed in this field.

7. Draft Status and Open Issues

This framework remains a work in progress. While the algebraic and analytic foundations of the Graph Reals and the Graph–Field Metric appear sound, several directions invite further development:

  • Precise characterization of the image of \( \mathbb{R}_G^\times \) inside \( \mathcal{B}(H)^2 \) under the scaled operator–pair embedding \( G \).
  • Identification of canonical copies of \( \mathbb{R} \subset \mathbb{R}_G \) via spectral or homomorphism-density data.
  • Interaction of the Graph Reals with homomorphism densities and subgraph-counting polynomials.
  • Extensions to signed, weighted, or directed graphs, and to hypergraph analogues.

The present document should be read as a cleaned-up scaffold: the Graph–Field Metric is now explicitly defined as a scaled operator–pair metric, the Graph Reals are constructed as its completion, and the resulting structure is proved to be a complete topological field. Further refinements will likely sharpen the interplay with graphons and spectral graph theory.
