Sidorenko Energy Along Ghost Lines: A Toy Model Result in Graph Reals

A small convexity result for Sidorenko-type energy inside the field of Graph Reals, using the ghost edge as a one-dimensional test direction.

Other entries in this series introduce the construction of Graph Reals \( \mathbb{R}_G \), the Graph–Field metric, and the ghost edge \( \mathbb{E}_\varnothing \). This note applies that machinery to a classical object from extremal graph theory: Sidorenko’s conjecture.

The goal here is modest but concrete: package Sidorenko’s inequality into a log-ratio that behaves like an “energy,” extend it to Graph Reals along the graphon slice, and then prove a simple convexity statement along one particular one-parameter family of Graph Reals, the ghost line.

1. Sidorenko’s Conjecture and Energy

Let \( H \) be a finite bipartite graph. For a graphon \( W:[0,1]^2 \to [0,1] \), the homomorphism density of \( H \) in \( W \) is

\[ t(H,W) = \int_{[0,1]^{V(H)}} \prod_{(i,j)\in E(H)} W(x_i,x_j)\,dx. \]

The edge density is the special case \[ t(K_2,W) = \int_{[0,1]^2} W(x,y)\,dx\,dy. \]

Sidorenko’s conjecture asserts that for each bipartite \( H \), every graphon \( W \) obeys \[ t(H,W) \;\ge\; t(K_2,W)^{e(H)}. \] The constant graphon \( W \equiv p \) gives equality.

Definition (Sidorenko energy). For a graphon \( W \) with \( t(H,W) > 0 \) and \( t(K_2,W) > 0 \), define the Sidorenko energy of \( H \) at \( W \) by \[ \mathcal{E}_H(W) := \log t(H,W) - e(H)\,\log t(K_2,W). \] Sidorenko’s inequality is equivalent to \[ \mathcal{E}_H(W) \ge 0. \]

In this logarithmic form, the conjecture says that the constant graphons minimize \( W \mapsto \mathcal{E}_H(W) \) among all graphons with a given edge density.

2. Graph Reals and the Graphon Slice

The Graph Reals \( \mathbb{R}_G \) arise by:

  • forming the graph rationals \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) from graph naturals and integers,
  • embedding \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) into bounded operators on a Hilbert space via an operator map \( \Psi \),
  • equipping \( \mathcal{Q}_G \) with the Graph–Field metric \( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \) built from \( \Psi(q) \) and its regularized inverse,
  • completing \( (\mathcal{Q}_G,d_{\mathrm{GF}}) \) to a topological field \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

Along a suitable “graphon slice” of \( \mathcal{Q}_G \), each element can be represented by a step kernel, hence by a graphon \( W \), and the operator \( \Psi(q) \) acts as the usual kernel operator \( T_W \) on \( L^2[0,1] \). Limits in \( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \) along this slice recover graphon limits in the cut metric and in operator norm.

Definition (Graphon-valued Graph Reals). A Graph Real \( X \in \mathbb{R}_G \) is called graphon-valued when the operator \( \Psi(X) \) arises from a symmetric kernel \( W_X:[0,1]^2\to[0,1] \) in the usual way. On these elements one sets \[ t(H,X) := t(H,W_X),\quad t(K_2,X) := t(K_2,W_X), \] and defines the Sidorenko energy \[ \mathcal{E}_H(X) := \log t(H,X) - e(H)\,\log t(K_2,X). \]

Graphon convergence in the cut metric follows from convergence in \( d_{\mathrm{GF}} \) along that slice, so the same formulas extend by continuity from graph rationals to the completed field.

3. The Ghost Edge and Ghost Line

The ghost edge \( \mathbb{E}_\varnothing \in \mathbb{R}_G \) is constructed as a Cauchy limit of suitably normalized cycle or clique graphs. It has three key properties:

  • \( v(\mathbb{E}_\varnothing) = 0 \) (zero vertex count),
  • \( e(\mathbb{E}_\varnothing) = 1 \) (unit edge count),
  • \( \Psi(\mathbb{E}_\varnothing) = 0 \) (zero kernel operator).

The last item is central for this note: kernel-level quantities are blind to ghost edges.

Definition (Ghost line). The ghost line in \( \mathbb{R}_G \) is the set \[ L_{\mathrm{ghost}} := \{ a\,\mathbb{E}_\varnothing : a \in \mathbb{R} \}. \] Each element of \( L_{\mathrm{ghost}} \) carries a pure edge count via \( e(a\,\mathbb{E}_\varnothing)=a \) and no vertex count or kernel.

For any \( X\in\mathbb{R}_G \) one can form the translated line \[ X_\lambda := X + \lambda\,\mathbb{E}_\varnothing, \] a one-parameter family parallel to the ghost line. At the level of counts, this changes the effective edge count while leaving the operator \( \Psi(X) \) unchanged.

4. A Toy Model Convexity Result: Sidorenko Energy Along Ghost Lines

The following statement uses the ghost line to produce a one-dimensional family of Graph Reals along which the Sidorenko energy stays completely flat. This gives a first, simple “geodesic” for Sidorenko energy inside \( \mathbb{R}_G \).

Theorem (Ghost-line convexity of Sidorenko energy). Let \( H \) be a bipartite graph. Let \( X \in \mathbb{R}_G \) be a graphon-valued Graph Real with \( t(H,X) > 0 \) and \( t(K_2,X) > 0 \). For each real parameter \( \lambda \), define \[ X_\lambda := X + \lambda\,\mathbb{E}_\varnothing. \] Then:
  1. Every \( X_\lambda \) has the same kernel operator as \( X \).
  2. The Sidorenko energy satisfies \[ \mathcal{E}_H(X_\lambda) = \mathcal{E}_H(X) \quad\text{for all }\lambda\in\mathbb{R}. \] As a function of \( \lambda \), the map \( \lambda \mapsto \mathcal{E}_H(X_\lambda) \) is constant, hence convex.

Proof

Step 1 (Kernel invariance). The operator embedding \( \Psi \) is linear, and the ghost edge satisfies \( \Psi(\mathbb{E}_\varnothing)=0 \). Hence \[ \Psi(X_\lambda) = \Psi(X + \lambda\,\mathbb{E}_\varnothing) = \Psi(X) + \lambda\,\Psi(\mathbb{E}_\varnothing) = \Psi(X). \] Every \( X_\lambda \) corresponds to the same kernel \( W_X \), so the underlying graphon is independent of \( \lambda \).

Step 2 (Homomorphism densities are ghost-blind). Since \( X_\lambda \) and \( X \) share the same kernel \( W_X \), the homomorphism densities coincide: \[ t(H,X_\lambda) := t(H,W_{X_\lambda}) = t(H,W_X) = t(H,X), \] and similarly for the edge density, \[ t(K_2,X_\lambda) := t(K_2,W_{X_\lambda}) = t(K_2,W_X) = t(K_2,X). \]

Step 3 (Energy independence). Plugging these identities into the definition of the Sidorenko energy gives \[ \mathcal{E}_H(X_\lambda) = \log t(H,X_\lambda) - e(H)\,\log t(K_2,X_\lambda) = \log t(H,X) - e(H)\,\log t(K_2,X) = \mathcal{E}_H(X) \] for every real \( \lambda \). This function of \( \lambda \) is constant and therefore convex on \( \mathbb{R} \). \(\square\)

5. Interpretation and Next Directions

The theorem shows that along each line parallel to the ghost axis, Sidorenko energy behaves in a very simple way: it stays fixed. From the point of view of the log-ratio \[ \mathcal{E}_H(X) = \log t(H,X) - e(H)\,\log t(K_2,X), \] the ghost direction acts as a genuine null direction.

Remark (Shape vs mass). The ghost component changes the effective edge count \( e(\cdot) \) and related size information in \( \mathbb{R}_G \), yet leaves the kernel \( W_X \) unchanged. Sidorenko energy depends only on the “shape” of this kernel, not on how much extra edge mass has been tucked into the ghost line.

In the scalar–ghost plane view, the constant kernels (Erdős–Rényi graphons) live along a scalar line, and ghost edges extend that line into a two-dimensional plane. The result above says that, once one fixes the underlying kernel, moving within the ghost direction has no effect on Sidorenko energy.

From a wider perspective, this suggests organizing Sidorenko-type questions inside \( \mathbb{R}_G \) on a reduced “shape space” obtained by modding out ghost translations (and, if desired, positive scalings of the kernel). Nontrivial convexity or extremality statements for \( \mathcal{E}_H \) on that reduced space would express genuine structure theorems for Sidorenko graphs in a Graph-Reals language.

This short note focuses on a single one-parameter family to illustrate how the ghost edge interacts with Sidorenko energy. The same framework invites further questions, such as:

  • geodesic convexity of \( \mathcal{E}_H \) along other paths inside the graphon slice of \( \mathbb{R}_G \), especially those interpolating between structured kernels and their constant baselines;
  • spectral reinterpretations of \( t(H,X) \) in terms of the operator \( \Psi(X) \), aimed at majorization-type inequalities for eigenvalues;
  • flows in \( \mathbb{R}_G \) that preserve edge density and drive \( X_t \) toward scalar–ghost baselines while sending the Sidorenko energy steadily downward.

Even though the theorem here is deliberately small, it shows that Sidorenko’s conjecture can be discussed directly inside the Graph Reals field and that the ghost edge singles out clean null directions for Sidorenko energy. This already hints that the richer geometry of \( \mathbb{R}_G \) might support deeper convexity or flow-based approaches to long-standing extremal problems.
