Graph Reals and Sidorenko's Inequality

This page outlines how operator-theoretic tools built into the Graph Reals framework can be used to prove Sidorenko-type inequalities. Specifically, we use operator convexity and spectral decomposition techniques to derive lower bounds on homomorphism densities purely from functional analysis, bypassing traditional combinatorial proofs.

1. Background: Sidorenko's Conjecture

Given a bipartite graph H with m edges, and a graphon W, Sidorenko's conjecture asserts: t(H,W)≥t(K2,W)m. While this inequality has been proven for many specific cases, a unified analytic derivation has remained elusive.

2. Graph Reals and Operator Embedding

In the Graph Reals framework, graphs are represented as positive regularized integral operators on L2([0,1]): TGf(x):=∫01WG(x,y)f(y)dy+δf(x), where WG is a block-constant kernel from the adjacency matrix of G, and δ>0 ensures strict positivity.

Formal graph quotients x/y are embedded via: Ψ(xy):=|Ty|−1/2Uy∗TxUy|Ty|−1/2, with Ty=Uy|Ty| the polar decomposition.

3. Operator Convexity and Jensen’s Inequality

Let TW be the operator arising from a graphon W, and suppose it admits a spectral decomposition: TW=∑rλrPr. Normalizing via S=TW/τ, we form the tensor power S⊗m, which is a convex combination of rank-one projections.

Applying Jensen’s inequality to the operator-convex function f(X)=Xm−1 yields: f(S⊗m)≤S⊗m. Rescaling back, we obtain: (TW⊗m)m−1≤τm(m−2)TW⊗m.

4. Homomorphism Densities as Traces

Each homomorphism density can be written as a trace: t(H,W)=Tr(RHTW⊗m), for a suitable test-operator RH that encodes the structure of H. When H is bipartite, these trace operators factor: RH=RK2⊗m. This enables us to express both sides of the inequality in terms of t(K2,W) and t(H,W).

5. Final Inequality and Bipartiteness

Plugging into the inequality: Tr(RH(TW⊗m)m−1)≤τm(m−2)Tr(RHTW⊗m), and factoring the trace via the bipartite structure, we get: t(K2,W)m−1t(H,W)≤τm(m−2)t(K2,W)m. Dividing through and assuming τ=1, we obtain: t(H,W)≥t(K2,W)m.

6. Why the Graph Reals Approach Streamlines the Proof

Classical approaches to Sidorenko-type inequalities often require measure-theoretic approximation, combinatorial decompositions, or entropy arguments that depend delicately on properties of step-functions or symmetry in bipartite graphs. By contrast, the Graph Reals framework embeds graphons and finite graphs alike into a space of bounded self-adjoint operators, where analytic tools become natural and powerful.

In this setting, the homomorphism density t(H,W) becomes a single trace on a tensor power of an operator TW, and inequalities like t(H,W)≥t(K2,W)m reduce to comparisons between tensor powers — exactly the kind of objects that operator convexity is designed to control.

The key technical advantages of the Graph Reals setting include:

  • Tensor-power reduction: Graph homomorphism densities are reinterpreted as traces over tensor powers of operators, eliminating the need for nested integrals over vertex tuples.
  • Access to operator convexity: Once in operator form, convexity of functions like X↦Xm−1 allows clean application of Jensen’s inequality without coordinate-level manipulations.
  • Polar decomposition and normalization: The quotient construction Ψ(x/y) encodes relative structure while eliminating scale sensitivity, allowing us to analyze the geometry of graphs through relative operators.
  • Metric control: The pair-operator norm dominates the usual cut-metric, so all graphon limits remain accessible via continuity arguments — even though the algebra is defined purely from finite graphs.
  • Compatibility with spectral tools: Decompositions of TW into projections yield convex combinations of rank-one terms, so the proof reduces to eigenvalue-wise arguments, bypassing complicated probabilistic estimates.

In sum, the Graph Reals let us replace combinatorial estimates with analytic structure, shifting the problem into a space where the inequality emerges as a direct corollary of spectral calculus. The bipartite assumption still plays a role — but only in justifying one clean multiplicative identity for trace functionals. The heavy lifting is now spectral, not combinatorial.

7. Generalization and Operator-Theoretic Insight

Interestingly, the operator inequality itself: Tr(R(TW⊗m)m−1)≤τm(m−2)Tr(RTW⊗m), holds for any positive trace-class operator R. Thus, this result extends far beyond graphons, offering an operator-analytic inequality with potential applications in quantum information, spectral theory, and beyond.

8. Draft Status and Future Work

This derivation is part of a larger exploration of how analytic techniques can recast classical extremal graph inequalities. The operator-convexity argument appears robust, but the structural conditions on RH and the exact mapping between densities and traces must be carefully verified. Like the Graph Reals framework itself, this is a conceptual foundation awaiting refinement.
